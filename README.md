## What this book is

A constitutional blueprint for the age of machine intelligence — written at the intersection of three traditions that rarely speak to each other: **legal scholarship, artificial intelligence research, and political philosophy.**

Where Isaac Asimov gave us *Three Laws of Robotics* as plot devices, this book offers **Seven Laws of Artificial Intelligence** as governance instruments — reasoned proposals for debate, refinement, and eventual adoption by jurisdictions worldwide.

It is written as if it might be read by:
- a **judge in 2050** ruling on machine personhood,
- a **policymaker** facing true artificial general intelligence, or
- a **historian** trying to understand why humanity did, or did not, preserve what mattered most about itself.

---

## Why read it

Because *Mata v. Avianca* (2023, S.D.N.Y.) showed us that AI hallucinations now appear in court filings. Because *Thaler v. Comptroller-General of Patents* [2023] UKSC 49 shut the door on AI inventorship without resolving who owns the output. Because *Loomis v. Wisconsin*, 881 N.W.2d 749 (Wis. 2016) let an algorithm influence a prison sentence without the defendant ever seeing its code. Because the *NYT v. OpenAI* litigation will not be the last of its kind.

The crisis is not coming. It has arrived. The law has not.

## What's inside

The book is divided into five parts:

| Part | Focus |
|------|-------|
| **I** | The philosophical foundations — consciousness, agency, dignity |
| **II** | Where existing law fails — liability, intent, personhood, jurisdiction |
| **III** | How modern AI actually learns, optimises, and behaves autonomously |
| **IV** | **The Mali Doctrines** — constitutional imperatives, not suggestions |
| **V** | **The Seven Laws of Artificial Intelligence** — reasoned proposals for adoption |

## A small jurisprudential note

The licence here is permissive on purpose. Locking up ideas about how law should govern intelligence — while pleading for openness from the machines themselves — would be, to borrow from H.L.A. Hart, a *pathology of the rule of recognition*. If the Doctrines are right, they should survive remix. If they are wrong, openness is the fastest path to discovering it.

*Caveat lector. Caveat auctor. And above all — caveat humanity.*
